There was no existing function to inherit. No stable operating model. No clean boundary between product, engineering, and commercial reality.

That sounds exciting when people say it quickly. In practice, it mostly means you make visible mistakes in public while trying to build standards and momentum at the same time.

The useful lesson was not "move fast." It was learning what has to be made real first.

## Start with decision quality, not headcount

In the beginning, the instinct was to prove value quickly by shipping visible work. Some of that was necessary. Some of it was expensive theater.

The better approach turned out to be slower and less flattering. Spend more time framing the problem. Be more selective about what gets built. Say no to projects that would create excitement but not durable capability. A system built to look productive in month one often has to be rebuilt by month three.

Building from zero means a lot of your early work is really about decision quality. What deserves effort. What can wait. What kind of standard you are willing to defend when the business wants speed.

## Hire for ambiguity tolerance

My biggest mistakes were in hiring.

I overvalued experience on paper and undervalued how people think when the problem is not clearly defined. That was costly, because the problem was almost never clearly defined. The people who worked out best were usually not the people with the cleanest resume. They were the ones who could stay calm around uncertainty, ask better questions, and avoid building before they understood what mattered.

That changed how I evaluate technical talent. I care less about whether someone has seen the exact stack before and more about whether they can reason under constraint without creating noise for everyone around them.

## Make standards visible early

One thing I underestimated was how much standards need to be made visible before a team feels busy.

Code review standards. How problems get framed. What counts as done. What gets documented. How tradeoffs get explained. Who owns quality when the deadline is already tight.

I used to think some of that would emerge naturally from good people doing good work. Some of it does. Most of it does not. If you do not name the standard early, the team will still create one. It just will not be the one you wanted.

## What changed my mind

Over time the function became something real. People could make technical decisions without waiting for me. Work could continue without turning every question into escalation. That was the actual goal.

The lesson I would carry forward is simple: when you are building capability from zero, culture is not a soft layer you add later. It is part of the operating system from the start.

If I did it again, I would spend more time on that earlier and less time trying to look fast.
