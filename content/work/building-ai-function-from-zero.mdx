There was no team to join. No architecture diagram on a whiteboard. No senior engineer to shadow for a month before touching anything real. Just a business that needed AI to work, and a gap where the capability was supposed to be.

Building from zero means there is no one to blame and no playbook to follow.

The first few months were mostly about figuring out what not to build. The instinct in early-stage environments is to prove yourself fast — ship something visible, show traction, justify the hire. I did some of that. But I also learned that a system built to impress in the first month usually needs to be rebuilt by the third. The pressure to look productive and the discipline to be productive are not the same thing.

Hiring was where I made my most consequential mistakes. I optimized for speed and experience on paper when I should have optimized for how people think under ambiguity. A few early hires looked great in interviews but struggled when the problem was not clearly defined — which was always. The ones who worked out were the ones who could sit with uncertainty, ask better questions, and resist the urge to build before they understood.

What "optimizing for durability" actually looked like: slower hiring, more time on problem framing before writing any code, saying no to projects that would produce demos but not systems. It also meant having uncomfortable conversations about quality when the business wanted speed. I got better at those conversations over time, but I was not naturally good at them.

The function eventually became something real — capable of supporting deployments instead of just experiments. People could make technical decisions without waiting for me. That was the point: build something that does not depend on any single person, including yourself. But it took longer than I expected, and the path was less clean than any retrospective makes it sound.

If I did it again, I would spend more time on culture earlier. I treated it as something that would emerge from good work, but culture is a decision you make before the work starts.
