## Context

The work sat inside a real hotel environment with 441 rooms, uneven occupancy, live operational constraints, and no tolerance for theatrical results. The goal was not to make an optimization dashboard look intelligent. The goal was to improve HVAC performance in a way that could be defended operationally and financially.

## Challenge

This is where a lot of AI energy claims fall apart. Large building systems are noisy. Occupancy changes. Weather changes. Equipment ages. Human behavior is inconsistent. If you are loose with your baseline or selective with your time windows, you can make almost any result look persuasive.

The hard part was not producing a good story. It was deciding what would count as a credible one.

## Judgment

The central decision was to bias toward rigor over marketability.

That meant being conservative about attribution, careful about measurement windows, and resistant to clean narratives that the data did not fully support. It also meant treating operations teams as critical collaborators instead of passive recipients of a model.

In practice, integrity was part of the system design.

## Approach

We treated the deployment like an operating system, not a slide. That meant grounding the model in live building behavior, working closely with the people responsible for comfort and reliability, and designing measurement in a way that could survive scrutiny after the fact.

We narrowed scope where needed. We separated signal from noise as carefully as possible. We paid attention to where the system could be directionally right but operationally unhelpful.

## Outcome

The most important outcome was not a marketing number. It was a stronger internal standard for what counted as believable optimization in a messy environment. The work proved that disciplined AI deployment can create value, but only if the team is honest about uncertainty and rigorous about evaluation.

That is less exciting than inflated savings claims. It is more useful.

## Reflection

Scale exposes weak thinking. The bigger the deployment, the harder it is to hide behind selective interpretation. This case reinforced a principle I keep: if a result cannot survive careful measurement, it should not be used as positioning.
